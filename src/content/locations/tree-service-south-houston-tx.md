South Houston is a small city with big trees. Its blocks were platted a century ago, so the lots are narrow, the houses are close, and the pecans, hackberries, and oaks that grew up between them now touch rooflines and service drops on both sides of the fence. We are the shop next door: Crenshaw Rd in Pasadena, minutes away.

## How do you remove a tree from a tight South Houston lot?

In pieces, from the top down. Most South Houston backyards have no gate wide enough for machinery, so the climber rigs each section, lowers it on ropes, and the wood goes out through the side yard by hand. It is slower than dropping a tree in an open field, and it is the only safe way here. When a storm has already put the tree on the house, the same crew runs it as an [emergency tree removal](/services/tree-removal/emergency-tree-removal/) with the phone answered around the clock Monday through Saturday.

## What about the stump in a yard this size?

On a small South Houston lot, the stump is the yard, so grinding is included with every removal we do. If you inherited stumps from someone else's work, [stump removal](/services/stump-grinding/stump-removal/) and [root removal](/services/stump-grinding/root-removal/) reclaim the ground for grass, a shed, or a garden bed.

## Can overgrown trees near the lines be trimmed safely?

Yes, with the clearances the utility expects. South Houston's overhead drops run close to a lot of canopies, and our [canopy trimming](/services/tree-trimming/canopy-trimming/) keeps limbs off your roof and away from your service line without topping the tree. The International Society of Arboriculture covers why proper pruning beats topping every time at [treesaregood.org](https://www.treesaregood.org/).
