Pasadena doesn't wonder whether storm season is coming — it's on the calendar, June through November, every year. The only real question is what your trees will do when it arrives. After Hurricane Beryl tore through the Houston area in 2024, most of the damage in the neighborhoods came from the same predictable sources: limbs that were already dead, branches stretched over rooflines, and crowns too dense to let the wind through.

All three are fixable in an afternoon of calm weather. That's the entire idea of storm preparation trimming — an arborist walks your property, identifies the wood the next storm would take, and takes it first, on a day when it lands in a chipper instead of on your shingles.

## What does a storm-prep trim include?

The direct answer: deadwood removal, canopy thinning, and roofline clearance. Dead limbs go because they're the first casualties of any wind. The crown gets selectively thinned so gusts pass through rather than pushing on it — a sail with holes in it. And everything overhanging the roof, driveway, and service drop gets cut back to safe clearance. The [National Weather Service's Houston/Galveston office](https://www.weather.gov/hgx/) preaches pre-season preparedness every spring; your trees are the biggest item on that checklist.

## Which trees should worry me most before a storm?

The ones with existing defects over targets — and Pasadena's usual suspects are water oaks with hidden trunk decay, tall pines that snap mid-trunk in sustained wind, and anything leaning since the last saturated spring.
