You cut the tree down — or paid someone to — and the yard still isn't yours. There's a stump where the new patio goes, or a ring of stubborn suckers where a crepe myrtle refuses to accept its own removal. When the roots are the problem, the fix has to go where the roots are.

Full stump removal takes out the entire root ball: the stump, the buttress roots, the mass of soil-bound wood under it. It's more invasive than grinding and priced accordingly, which is exactly why we'll tell you at the free estimate when you don't need it — most Pasadena stumps are perfectly served by standard [stump grinding](/services/stump-grinding/), and we'd rather quote you the cheaper right answer.

## When is full removal worth the extra cost?

When something is going in that spot that roots would ruin — a slab, a driveway, footings, a pool, or a new tree planted in the same hole. Decaying roots left under new concrete settle into voids; a sapling planted into an old root mass fights for years. Texas A&M AgriLife's [planting guidance](https://agrilifeextension.tamu.edu/) is clear that new trees want clean soil, and construction wants ground that won't sink.

## What happens to the hole afterward?

You choose the finish. We can backfill and rough-grade the excavation so it's ready for sod, leave it open and clean for your contractor's forms, or blend it into a bed you're rebuilding. Everything pulled out leaves with us — root mass, soil spoil, the works — through the same [debris removal](/services/land-clearing/debris-removal/) that finishes our tree jobs.

Removal also stacks efficiently with bigger projects: pulling root systems is routine inside a [lot clearing](/services/land-clearing/lot-clearing/), and when live roots are invading pipes or lifting concrete near a tree you're keeping, targeted [root removal](/services/stump-grinding/root-removal/) solves it without excavating anything else.
