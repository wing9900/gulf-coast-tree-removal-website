Look up into your trees. Gray, bare branches threaded through the green? Limbs crossing and rubbing bark off each other? Growth so dense the inside of the canopy never dries out? That's a tree quietly building problems — and every one of those problems eventually lands in the yard, usually during a storm.

Pruning removes them on your schedule instead of the weather's. And it has to be done right, because a bad cut is permanent: a certified arborist takes each branch back to the collar where the tree can compartmentalize the wound, never leaves stubs, and never "tops" a tree — the fastest way to turn a healthy canopy into a hazard factory.

## What actually gets removed when you prune?

Deadwood first — it serves the tree nothing and gravity everything. Then structural corrections: crossing limbs, competing leaders, weak V-shaped attachments that split under load, and water sprouts stealing energy. The target is a canopy that light and wind move through cleanly. In Pasadena's humidity that airflow matters double, since a congested crown holds the moisture that fungal problems love.

## Why does oak timing matter so much here?

Because oak wilt is fatal and Texas is its home turf. The disease rides sap-feeding beetles to fresh cuts, so [Texas A&M Forest Service guidance](https://tfsweb.tamu.edu/) — and our own rule — is to prune live oaks and red oaks in the coldest months and seal any wound that can't wait. It's a detail a mow-and-blow crew won't know and your oaks can't survive them not knowing.
