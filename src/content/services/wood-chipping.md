Cut a tree apart and you learn a strange fact: the branches take up ten times the room of the trunk. A single mature Pasadena oak makes a brush pile that swallows a driveway — which is why the chipper, not the chainsaw, is the machine that actually gives you your yard back.

Chipping happens on site, the day of the work. Limbs go in whole, chips come out at a fraction of the volume, and you make one simple choice: keep them or lose them.

## Is fresh wood-chip mulch actually good for my landscaping?

Yes — arborist chips are some of the best mulch you can put down, and yours are free because you grew them. A few inches of chips over beds suppresses weeds, holds soil moisture through Pasadena's brutal summers, and feeds the soil as they break down; Texas A&M AgriLife's [mulching guidance](https://agrilifeextension.tamu.edu/) recommends exactly this kind of coarse organic layer. Two honest caveats from the arborist: keep chips pulled back from trunks and stems, and skip chips from any tree removed for disease.

## What happens to the chips I don't want?

They leave with us — chipping and [debris removal](/services/land-clearing/debris-removal/) are two halves of the same cleanup, and the chipper is what makes hauling affordable by shrinking the load before it's loaded. That combination finishes every job we do: the canopy from a [large tree removal](/services/tree-removal/large-tree-removal/), the cuttings from [canopy trimming](/services/tree-trimming/canopy-trimming/), the mountains of material a [lot clearing](/services/land-clearing/lot-clearing/) produces. Trunk wood too big to chip gets bucked for firewood if you want it or hauled if you don't.
