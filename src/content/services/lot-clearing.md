An overgrown lot doesn't just sit there — it costs you. Buyers can't see past the brush, builders can't stake a slab in it, the city's code enforcement notices it, and every month it stands, the volunteer trees get bigger and the clearing bill grows with them.

Clearing it is a sequencing job, and doing it out of order is how rental-equipment weekends end in a half-cleared mess. Our sequence is fixed: walk it, flag it, drop it, process it, grind it, haul it, done.

## What does "cleared" actually mean when the crew leaves?

You define it before we start, in writing. Cleared-to-mow means brush and small trees gone, keepers standing, ground rough-leveled. Cleared-to-build means everything inside the footprint removed including root systems — that's where full [stump removal](/services/stump-grinding/stump-removal/) enters — with the rest of the lot selectively cleared around it. Either way, mature live oaks and healthy pines you want kept are marked and protected; an arborist-run clearing crew's advantage is knowing a $10,000 shade tree from a weed with a trunk.

## What's the difference between lot clearing and just brush hogging?

Brush hogging is one tool inside the job — the machine pass that levels the undergrowth. Lot clearing is the whole outcome: [brush hogging](/services/land-clearing/brush-hogging/) the thick stuff, taking down unwanted trees (including the [large ones](/services/tree-removal/large-tree-removal/) that need real rigging near property lines), [chipping](/services/land-clearing/wood-chipping/) what can become mulch, grinding the stumps flush, and [hauling the debris](/services/land-clearing/debris-removal/) that shouldn't stay. If a lot only needs the mow-down, we'll quote you the smaller job honestly.

One more Pasadena-specific note: lots near the bayous and drainage ways carry easement rules, and the [Harris County Flood Control District](https://www.hcfcd.org/) cares what happens along its channels. We've worked around these systems for years — you find out about a restriction from us at the estimate, not from an inspector after the fact.
